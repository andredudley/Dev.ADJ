Fighting Games, And The Importance of Learning How to Suck.

February 15, 2026

I love fighting games.

It's one of my favorite genres of games ever. But, with that being said:

Fighting games are ruthless.

There is nothing quite like the feeling of picking up a character, learning combos in training mode, hopping online... and getting bodied in 23 seconds. I may or may not speak from experience.

That feeling of seeing nothing work for you, and everything work for the guy (or girl) absolutely bodying you. Now, a humiliating 17-hit combo, a "You Lose" screen, and a bruised ego later, there is a voice in the back of your head saying:

Maybe fighters just aren't for me.

That voice... is absolutely LYING. But it took me 15 years, multiple fighting games across multiple console generations, and a flippin' Computer Science degree to finally understand why that voice is lying.

I simply didn't know how to be bad at things. It sounds nutty, but just hear me out.

Learning how to be bad at things—genuinely horrible, at a humiliating level—is truly the only path to being good. Whether you're picking up fighters for the first time, or learning to code, mastery is not just miraculously achieved. People, especially in this day and age, have seemed to forget what being a novice is.



Ground Zero

With mastery, comes humble beginnings, and I do not think beginnings could be any humbler than my first ever fighter:

Mortal Kombat vs. DC Universe

But, you have to understand: I'm, like, seven years old at this point. I was enthralled by seeing Batman fighting some magical fire and ice ninjas. I had no idea that those ninjas were the posterboys of one of the most iconic fighting game franchises in history, it was just seven year old me having a blast spamming batarangs and looking up tutorials for what to do when the big red "FINISH HIM" popped up on the screen when I won.

In retrospect, it is a miracle I stuck with the genre with a game like that being my introduction to the genre. But I will say that the character design in that game, minus Jax, was chef's kiss. Sometimes, your introduction to something like fighting games is a janky, but endearing game like MK vs. DC, or an incredibly annoying IDE (I'm looking at you Eclipse). But, this doesn't mean that the "maybe this isn't for me" voice should win.

This doesn't mean that you are inherently bad, and doomed to stay that way forever. You just haven't come across the good version of what you're supposed to be learning yet.

Thankfully, I grew up in a household with two parents that absolutely loved fighters. And, with that, I was introduced to the majesty of TEKKEN 6.

I Was Hooked

A new game with fun controls, deep combat mechanics, tons of content, and a massive roster that contains who would become my favorite fighting game character ever:

King

There was something that clicked in my ten-year-old mind when I began to main King. Whether it was the beginning of my process of picking a main in fighting games (picking the character I think looks cool), or the fact he literally roared under his jaguar mask, my ten-year-old brain had decided that this was undoubtedly the coolest thing ever. And he wrestles??? This became more than some silly game with Batman fighting a bunch of ninjas—I was invested. It went from a fun little game to "I need to know every single multi-throw combo this man is capable of". This was it for me. The true "so this is what it's all about" moment.

I feel like every developer, especially those starting out, should have a "King" moment. That moment where you think "so this is what it's all about". Maybe it is a project, a static site generator, a framework, or maybe even a language like HTML or CSS. I still remember the first time CSS Grid really clicked for me. Granted, I'm still not great at it, but seeing how I could arrange a web page any way I wanted? I was hooked entirely.

I know I have more things to talk about, but can we talk about King real quick?

In a franchise filled with mega-corporations, robots, ninjas, demons, multiple bears, some goofy dude with a sword, multiple capoeira masters, a pair of femme fatale sisters, and martial artists of all sorts, here's a man who wrestles in a jaguar mask, and communicates with growls and roars.
That's it. That's all.
No superpowers, no fancy martial arts, no billion dollar corporations, no demonic entities hellbent on world domination, just a man who throws hands and Giant Swings without prejudice, in the name of the Lord and his orphanage.
And there are TWO of them!
The first King, King I, was the predecessor to the King we see in Tekkens 3-8. He even finishes third in the inaugural Tekken tournament while, get this, being an ordained priest. Tell me that isn't just the coolest thing you've ever heard.

Anyways, back to losing.

The Humbling

Tekken was a lot of fun, and as I got older, I caught wind of a game that my dad once called the "real Mortal Kombat".

Mortal Kombat 9

This was my first true taste of Mortal Kombat that didn't involve my favorite comic book heroes. And I quickly learned why that is.

While seven-year-old me was saved from the trauma of watching Superman get disemboweled by mythical warriors, thirteen-year-old me became occupied by the cool fire ninja from the past and the. . . interesting female character design. It was definitely a time to be alive as a Mortal Kombat fan.

And, with that, my lifelong commitment of being a Scopion main truly began. Not only that, but now I was competing. And, by "competing", I mean getting absolutely molly-whopped by my family. Whether it was my dad, cousins who were also fighting game fanatics, or that one cousin who just spammed that braindead teleport slam with Noob Saibot, I couldn't buy a win.

They were whipping out combos, mixups, frame traps, and counters that I never even knew existed. They punished just about every single mistake I made. I wasn't even close. And the worst part? It hardly looked like they were trying when they beat me.

It was, in one word, frustrating. Embarrassing. You could even venture to say humiliating. The urge to put the controller down (or throw it across the room), and just say "well, I didn't even like this game anyways" was incredibly strong.

This was my first ever "real world" experience with fighting games. That moment, despite all you've learned, where you realize "I don't know as much as I thought I did". When it came to web development, I remember the first time I attempted to create a responsive design for this blog and it looked like a grenade went off in my phone. Fun times (I'm lying). It was the moment I began to realize I wasn't exactly a pro at this kind of stuff, and that hint of doubt began to raise its voice again.

"Hm, maybe I'm not really cut out for this kind of stuff."

But here's the thing about cousins: they don't exactly go away.

They come back. They play again. I kept playing them. And something began to change.

I got better. Granted, I still got my butt kicked. A lot. But, it went from desperate button mashing and not knowing what's happening, to actually learning why I lost. From being taken as a joke, to being taken seriously. Even considered as a threat. A slight hint of dread, even, when I was the answer to the question "Who's Next?"

Mortal Kombat X

If Mortal Kombat 9 was my introduction to Mortal Kombat, Mortal Kombat X was the game that truly turned me into a fighting game player. I'm in my mid-to-late-teens at this point, and now I'm not just playing the game—I am actively studying. This was the first fighting game I can remember actually labbing properly in.

For those who don't know, labbing is essentially spending time in practice mode to hone combos, counters, move sets, frame data, etc. And given the sheer amount of movesets in this game thanks to the introduction of variations, there was a lot of time spent labbing.

With that being said, I was still losing. A lot.

But, I was also winning. And, I was winning a ton. Grudge matches with cousins finally became squash matches in my favor. The frustration of losing was turned into focus on mistakes I would make.

Maybe I overused a move that's negative on block and got rightfully punished for it.
Maybe I grew impatient and blew meter when I didn't need to.
Maybe I grew impatient and it cost me a round.
Maybe I just ran into someone better, and I should steal a page from their book of combos.

I finally realized that I didn't need to prove that I was good anymore. I just needed to understand why I wasn't. And that, for all intents and purposes, is what makes the path worth it in my opinion.

As a fighting game dork, and a web developer, I guess you can say that this revelation is the most important one you could ever possibly realize. I would come up with the most ridiculous Ninjutsu Scorpion, Covert Ops Sonya Blade, and Ethereal Mileena combos I could branch from a certain mixup.

I would toss tutorials to the wind, forgoing copying code line by line in favor of learning the basics. Asking the question of why it works. Reading documentation of languages like HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, or generators like 11ty (that was an entire can of worms on its own).

I break things on purpose now.

I Blow up my CSS, break my JavaScript, use different semantics in my HTML just to see what happens. I still write buggy, genuinely abhorrent code from time to time. But, now I can debug it. I can fix it. That helpless feeling leaves as well, but it isn't entirely because I'm winning.

It's because I understand the game now.

The Ongoing Match

I'm twenty-two years old now, and I've played my fair share of fighters. Different games, different systems, different speeds, different skillsets. Some stuck, others didn't. But, I'm not in that constant state of "beginner's anguish" I was once in. I'm hardly playing to be the best anymore, life's too short for that. I'm here for the love of the game.

There's something liberating about the feeling of knowing you have nothing to prove to anybody. Especially over something as trivial as a game. I'm at the point where I know that I am not going to be an expert off the rip. As a matter of fact, I'll more than likely suck. I did in Guilty Gear, I did in BlazBlue, I did in Street Fighter, I did in CSS, and I did in JavaScript, I did in just about any and everything you can think of.

And the thing that kept me going in the dog days of being hot garbage at the game?

Knowing it is temporary. I've been in this spot before. I've been a novice before. And I know how to grow from experience, not just winning.

As a man who has definitely had his fair share of tension headaches from responsive design, take it from me: this helpless feeling of not knowing what to do, or how to do it, will not last. That feeling of "maybe I'm just not cut out for this" shall pass as well. I know how to grow because I know the value of being awful at something. And I feel that many people, regardless of field or position, could also learn a thing or two about sucking at something.

So, my dear friend, if you find yourself stuck on a layout, a combo, or a concept you just can't quite grasp yet—good.

Keep breaking.

Keep mashing.

Keep failing.

Keep learning.

The foundation of mastery is incompetence. And I can firmly assure you, as a man who has lost more rounds in embarrassing fashion than I can ever begin to count: that trial by fire is beyond worth it.

And, think about it this way: if I had never found the strange and janky beauty of MK vs. DC as a kid? Those awkward "Close Combat" and "Freefall Kombat" mechanics? That absolutely busted "Rage" mechanic? I may have never found King. Might have never learned what I needed from MK9 and MKX. Might have never learned the valuable lesson that being bad at something wasn't the end of the world—but merely step one.

So here's to bad games. Worse code. Terrible starts. Abysmal beginnings.

Here's to sucking, and thugging it out anyways.
